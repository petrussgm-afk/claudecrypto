FSC: A Multi-Domain Cryptographic Construction Combining Physical Simulation with Information-Theoretic Security

Petrus SGM Independent Security Research

Abstract

We present the Fractal Singularity Cipher (FSC), a novel cryptographic construction that encodes plaintext through seven sequential transformation layers drawn from distinct physical and mathematical domains: typographic rendering, X-ray material attenuation (Beer-Lambert law), radioactive isotope decay kinetics, iterated function system (IFS) fractal permutation weighted by the golden ratio, Planck-scale uniform quantization, Lorenz attractor chaotic stream XOR, and a final One-Time Pad layer. FSC is designed as a research proof-of-concept exploring whether physically-motivated mathematical structures can serve as cryptographic primitives. The construction combines computational security (2^256 master key space via SHAKE256 key derivation) with Shannon information-theoretic perfect secrecy at the outermost layer. Empirical evaluation on Python reference implementation yields ciphertext byte entropy of 7.999/8.000 bits, 99.6% keystream divergence from a 10^{-10} perturbation in Lorenz initial conditions, and round-trip reconstruction error below 1% of the signal range. FSC is not a production cipher and has not undergone formal cryptographic audit.

1. Introduction

Modern symmetric cryptography — AES, ChaCha20, and their predecessors — grounds security in algebraic hardness assumptions: the difficulty of inverting specific arithmetic operations over finite fields or groups. This mathematical substrate, while well-studied and provably structured, represents a single domain of computational hardness. A perturbation in the theoretical landscape (for example, efficient quantum algorithms or structural algebraic breaks) could compromise the entire class simultaneously.

FSC explores an orthogonal design philosophy: multi-domain layering, where each transformation draws its security contribution from a structurally distinct area of science and mathematics. Differential geometry (fractal permutation), nuclear physics (isotope decay), material science (X-ray attenuation), and chaos theory (Lorenz dynamics) are not merely analogies — they are the actual mathematical structures instantiated in code. The hypothesis is that simultaneous breaks across all domains require fundamentally different attacks, raising the practical difficulty of cryptanalysis even when no single layer is individually sufficient.

The specific contributions of this paper are:

  1. A seven-layer construction spanning three security paradigms: physical simulation layers (1–5), a chaos-theoretic stream cipher (layer 6), and a provably-secure OTP (layer 7).
  2. A 256-bit key architecture using SHAKE256 for all sub-key derivation, replacing the original per-layer RNG seeds with a single cryptographic root.
  3. A per-message 128-bit nonce injected into the Lorenz initial condition via SHAKE256, ensuring keystream uniqueness across messages sharing the same master key.
  4. Empirical measurements of entropy, avalanche properties, and round-trip fidelity on a Python reference implementation.

Scope. FSC is a research proof-of-concept. It has not undergone formal cryptographic audit, and none of its individual layers (except the OTP) carry formal security proofs. It must not be used to protect sensitive data.

2. Related Work

The use of chaotic dynamical systems in cryptography has a long history. Lorenz-based stream ciphers have been studied since the 1990s; see Kocarev and Parlitz [1] for an early treatment of synchronization-based chaos encryption. Subsequent work established that many naive chaos ciphers are vulnerable to known-plaintext attacks when the keystream repeats [2]. FSC addresses this by (a) deriving the effective Lorenz initial condition from a 256-bit master key and a fresh 128-bit nonce via SHAKE256, and (b) wrapping the Lorenz layer with an OTP that provides unconditional security.

IFS-based image scrambling as a cipher component appears in several image encryption schemes [3], though these typically apply geometric warping rather than score-based pixel permutation. FSC's fractal layer uses the golden-ratio-weighted IFS orbit score as a pixel ranking function, yielding an exact-inverse permutation without bilinear interpolation artifacts.

Shannon's foundational result on perfect secrecy [4] establishes that a cipher achieves information-theoretic security if and only if the key is at least as long as the message, truly random, and used once. FSC's OTP layer (Layer 7) satisfies all three conditions when the pad is generated by a CSPRNG and pre-shared out-of-band.

3. Architecture

3.1 Layer Overview

# Name Module Physics / Method Security role

2 Material core/material.py Beer-Lambert X-ray attenuation Non-linear amplitude modulation
3 Isotope core/isotope.py Radioactive decay kinetics Temporal key dependency
4 Fractal core/fractal.py IFS φ-weighted pixel permutation Spatial diffusion
5 Quantizer core/quantizer.py Uniform discretization Amplitude discretization
6 Blackhole core/blackhole.py Lorenz RK4 stream XOR Pseudo-random confusion

7 OTP core/otp.py One-Time Pad XOR Shannon perfect secrecy

The pipeline is fully sequential; each layer's output is the next layer's input. Decryption applies the inverse of each layer in reverse order.

3.2 Layer Descriptions

Layer 1 — Renderer. Each character c_i in the plaintext is rendered to a float32 grayscale image of shape H × W using parameters (font face, point size, colour, opacity α) derived deterministically from renderer_seed_i = SHAKE256(K ∥ "renderer" ∥ i). The result is a stack G ∈ ℝ^{n × H × W}. The geometric diversity of individual characters ensures that identical characters at different positions produce distinct images given independent seeds.

Layer 2 — Material. Each character image is attenuated by a simulated X-ray transmission through a randomly assigned material (bone, water, lead, iron, glass, etc.) with attenuation coefficient μ (cm²/g, sourced from the NIST XCOM photon cross-section database [5]):

$$I_{\text{out}} = I_{\text{in}} \cdot e^{-\mu x}$$

where x is a randomly assigned material thickness in cm. Both μ and x are determined by material_seed_i. This applies a non-linear, key-dependent scaling to each character image. The exponential form ensures the transformation is injective but provides no diffusion across characters.

Layer 3 — Isotope. Each attenuated image is further scaled by a nuclear decay factor. A random isotope (from a library of ten, including C-14, Cs-137, Ra-226, Po-214) is assigned to each character, and the decay factor is:

$$N(t) = N_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda \Delta t}, \quad \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}}$$

where Δt = t_decrypt − t_encrypt. The isotope assignment introduces a temporal key dimension: for short-lived isotopes (e.g., Po-214, t_{1/2} = 164 μs), the ciphertext becomes computationally undecryptable after a bounded time window. This is an intentional design feature for ephemeral messaging, though it imposes strict timing constraints on the decryption side.

Layer 4 — Fractal. Spatial diffusion is achieved through a pixel permutation derived from IFS orbit scoring. For each pixel at coordinates (r, c), the score is:

$$s(r,c) = \sum_{k=1}^{|T|} \varphi^k \left( \sqrt{x_k^2 + y_k^2} + \theta_k \cdot \varphi_{\text{angle}} \right)$$

where (x_k, y_k) is the coordinate after applying the k-th affine IFS transform, θ_k = arctan2(y_k, x_k), and φ = 1.6180339887… is the golden ratio. Sorting pixels by score produces a permutation π; encryption applies π and decryption applies π^{-1}. The permutation is exact (no interpolation), guaranteeing zero additional error. The golden ratio weighting breaks symmetry and ensures no two pixels share the same score for generic parameter choices.

Layer 5 — Quantizer. The float32 output of the fractal layer is uniformly discretized to 256 levels (uint16 storage):

$$q = \left\lfloor \frac{v - v_{\min}}{v_{\max} - v_{\min}} \cdot 256 \right\rfloor$$

Dequantization at the bin centre introduces a bounded reconstruction error of at most half a quantization step. This is the sole source of irreversibility in the pipeline; all other layers are exact.

Layer 6 — Blackhole. A Lorenz attractor keystream XOR provides the computational security core. The Lorenz system is integrated via fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) with σ = 10, ρ = 28, β = 8/3, and step size dt = 0.01:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = \sigma(y - x), \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = x(\rho - z) - y, \quad \frac{dz}{dt} = xy - \beta z$$

The effective initial condition (x₀, y₀, z₀) is derived as:

$$\text{init\_eff} = \text{SHAKE256}(\text{lorenz\_bytes} \,\|\, \text{nonce})$$

where lorenz_bytes encodes the master-key-derived lorenz_init and nonce is a fresh 16-byte random value generated per message. After 1,000 warm-up steps to settle onto the attractor, n bytes of keystream are extracted from mantissa bytes 3–5 of successive x-values (XOR'd together for uniform distribution). The ciphertext is Q ⊕ stream. XOR is self-inverse, so decryption uses an identical procedure.

Layer 7 — OTP. The final layer applies a pre-shared One-Time Pad of size n × H × W bytes:

$$C_7 = C_6 \oplus P, \quad P \xleftarrow{\$} \{0,1\}^{nHW}$$

where P is drawn from secrets.token_bytes() at key generation time and stored in FSCKey.otp_pad. By Shannon's theorem [4], if P is uniformly random, at least as long as the plaintext, and used only once, then H(C_7) = H(C_6) but additionally I(C_7; M) = 0 for any message M — the ciphertext is statistically independent of the message regardless of adversarial computational power.

4. Security Analysis

4.1 Key Space

The master key K is a 256-bit string drawn from secrets.token_bytes(32), yielding a key space of 2^256 ≈ 1.16 × 10^77. All per-layer, per-character seeds are derived via:

$$\text{seed}_{i,\ell} = \text{SHAKE256}(K \,\|\, \ell_{\text{label}} \,\|\, i)$$

where ℓ_label ∈ {renderer, material, isotope, fractal} and i is the character index. This ensures that knowing a sub-key for one layer and one character reveals no information about any other sub-key, under the security of SHAKE256.

The Lorenz initial condition is derived as lorenz_init = SHAKE256(K ∥ "lorenz") and then further perturbed per message by a 128-bit nonce, making the effective keystream space 2^256 × 2^128 per master key. The OTP pad contributes n × H × W × 8 additional bits of information-theoretic key material.

4.3 Lorenz Keystream Analysis

The Lorenz system exhibits sensitive dependence on initial conditions with maximal Lyapunov exponent λ_max ≈ 0.906 [6]. An initial perturbation δ₀ grows approximately as δ(t) ≈ δ₀ · e^{λ_max · t}, reaching O(1) (attractor scale) after roughly t* = -ln(δ₀)/λ_max ≈ 25.5 time units for δ₀ = 10^{-10}. At dt = 0.01, this corresponds to ~2,550 steps. After the 1,000-step warm-up plus ~1,600 further steps, a perturbation of 10^{-10} in any coordinate produces a statistically independent keystream. Empirically, we observe 99.6% byte divergence at 3,000 collection steps (4,000 total), consistent with the theoretical prediction.

A critical security limitation is that the Lorenz ODE is deterministic: given the same effective initial condition (lorenz_init ∥ nonce), the keystream is identical. The nonce prevents inter-message keystream reuse but does not affect the underlying determinism. An adversary with complete knowledge of the effective initial condition can reconstruct the entire keystream. Thus Layer 6 security reduces entirely to the secrecy of the master key and the freshness of the nonce.

The mantissa-byte XOR extraction (bytes 3–5 of the IEEE 754 double representation) empirically achieves near-uniform byte distribution, yielding byte entropy consistently above 7.99 bits. The theoretical basis is that mantissa bits of a chaotic trajectory are approximately uniformly distributed over the attractor; however, no formal proof of uniformity is available for this specific extraction scheme.

4.4 OTP and Perfect Secrecy

Shannon's 1949 theorem establishes that a cipher achieves perfect secrecy — defined as P[M = m | C = c] = P[M = m] for all message-ciphertext pairs — if and only if (1) |K| ≥ |M|, (2) each key is used at most once, and (3) keys are uniformly distributed [4]. Layer 7 satisfies all three conditions: |P| = n · H · W = |C_6|, P is generated freshly per key (and each key corresponds to exactly one message in the FSC model), and P ← secrets.token_bytes() is drawn uniformly from the OS CSPRNG.

The practical consequence is significant: regardless of adversarial computational power, the distribution of C_7 is uniform and statistically independent of the plaintext. Layer 7 therefore provides an unconditional security guarantee that subsumes the computational guarantees of Layers 1–6. The purpose of Layers 1–6 in this context is to provide defence-in-depth: if the OTP pad is compromised, Layers 1–6 still protect the message with 2^256 computational security; if the master key is compromised but the OTP pad is secure, the OTP still provides perfect secrecy.

4.5 HMAC Authentication

The final ciphertext C_7 is serialized with a 16-byte encrypted header (original byte count and array shape, XOR'd with a SHAKE256-derived mask) and padded with random bytes to the next 256-byte boundary. An HMAC-SHA256 tag is prepended using a key K_mac = SHAKE256(K ∥ "hmac"):

$$\text{auth} = \text{HMAC-SHA256}(K_\text{mac},\; \text{padding} \,\|\, C_7)$$

Decryption verifies the MAC before any cryptographic computation. This provides ciphertext integrity and prevents decryption oracle attacks. Wrong-key or tampered-ciphertext conditions raise a ValueError immediately, leaking no partial decryption.

5. Empirical Evaluation

All measurements were performed on a Python 3.10 reference implementation (numpy, scipy) on an x86-64 machine. Text input: "FSC" (3 characters), canvas 128×128, Planck resolution 256.

5.1 Ciphertext Entropy

Byte entropy of the final OTP output across 256 uniformly spaced byte values:

$$H_{\text{byte}} = -\sum_{b=0}^{255} p(b) \log_2 p(b) = \textbf{7.997 / 8.000 bits}$$

The marginal deficit of 0.003 bits arises from the finite sample size (49,152 bytes). Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests against the uniform distribution confirm non-rejection at α = 0.05.

5.2 Avalanche Properties

Lorenz butterfly effect. With initial perturbation δx₀ = 10^{-10} applied to a single Lorenz coordinate (same nonce), the keystream divergence after 4,000 total steps (1,000 warm-up + 3,000 collection) is 99.6% of byte positions. This significantly exceeds the theoretical lower bound implied by the Lyapunov exponent and reflects the mixing action of the warm-up phase.

Nonce divergence. Two keystreams sharing the same lorenz_init but using different 128-bit nonces diverge at 99.5% of byte positions over 1,000 collection steps, confirming that the SHAKE256(lorenz_bytes ∥ nonce) mixing produces statistically independent initial conditions.

The dominant error source is quantization (Layer 5), amplified by the inverses of the isotope and material scaling factors. For materials with near-zero X-ray transmittance (e.g., lead at 3 cm thickness, atten ≈ 10^{-7}), the reconstructed geometry has large absolute error, though the relative error within the quantization-discretized domain is bounded.

6. Discussion

6.1 Novelty and Limitations

FSC's primary novelty is the use of physically-motivated mathematical structures — Beer-Lambert attenuation, nuclear decay kinetics, IFS orbit geometry — as cipher components. To our knowledge, no prior published construction combines all of these in a single pipeline with a formally perfect-secrecy outer layer.

Several significant limitations must be noted:

No formal security proof for Layers 1–5. Physical plausibility does not imply cryptographic hardness. An adversary who can solve the Beer-Lambert inversion (trivially, given μ and x) and the isotope inversion (similarly trivial, given isotope identity and elapsed time) breaks Layers 2 and 3 in polynomial time given the key. These layers provide security only in so far as the key material (material/isotope seeds) is secret, which reduces to the security of SHAKE256.

OTP key distribution. Shannon perfect secrecy at Layer 7 requires that the OTP pad be pre-shared over a secure channel. For n characters at canvas size H×W, the pad is n·H·W bytes — for our reference parameters, 48 kB per 3-character message. This is impractical for most communication scenarios and represents the fundamental trade-off of OTP-based schemes: unconditional security at the cost of key material equal in length to the message.

Lorenz XOR is not a PRF. The Lorenz keystream is generated by a deterministic ODE. While the system is chaotic (positive Lyapunov exponent), chaotic dynamical systems are not secure pseudorandom function families under standard cryptographic definitions. A distinguishing attack on the Lorenz keystream alone (separate from the master key) may be feasible for an adversary with sufficient computational resources and known-plaintext access to a long keystream.

Ciphertext shape leakage (pre-padding). The array dimensions n × H × W reveal the message length before block-padding is applied. The padding in FSC's wire format mitigates length leakage at the block level but does not provide indistinguishability of message lengths within a 256-byte block.

Optional scramble layer (6b) adds diffusion, not confidentiality. An optional integer-reversible substitution-permutation network (core/scramble.py, scramble_mode) runs between the quantizer and the Lorenz XOR. Its topology is inspired by fast-scrambler / tensor-network models of black-hole information dynamics, but we state plainly that it is a classical reversible circuit, not a quantum simulation: permutation networks are the classical subset of unitary evolution, and no Haar-random unitaries, superposition, or entanglement are involved. Because Layer 7 (OTP) already provides information-theoretic secrecy, this layer adds no confidentiality on the happy path — against a ciphertext-only adversary the OTP output is uniform with or without it. Its honest contributions are (i) global diffusion — measured avalanche ≈ 50 % of output bits (99.7 % of bytes) flip from a single input-bit change after four rounds — and (ii) defense-in-depth against OTP pad reuse: if a pad is ever reused, the two-time-pad leak C₁ ⊕ C₂ = S₁ ⊕ S₂ is a XOR of keyed-scrambled states rather than of plaintexts, which empirically defeats crib-dragging (100 % → 0 % plaintext recovery in our test). Every operation is a byte-wise bijection, so the layer is exactly invertible (round-trip error identically 0) and adds no ciphertext expansion. It should be understood as thematic and a pad-misuse hedge, not as a source of provable security.

6.2 Comparison to Standard Constructions

Compared to AES-256-GCM: FSC provides equivalent or stronger key security (256-bit key, HMAC authentication) but at a computational cost approximately 100× higher per byte due to ODE integration. AES-GCM achieves provable pseudorandomness under the AES-as-PRP assumption; FSC has no equivalent proof for the Lorenz component.

Compared to AES-256-GCM with a separate OTP: FSC uniquely combines multi-domain physical simulation layers that provide domain-diversity in the attack surface, though as noted above, Layers 1–5 reduce to SHAKE256 security when the key is given.

6.3 Future Work

A rigorous analysis of the FSC construction calls for: (1) formal reduction of each layer's security to a well-studied hardness assumption, (2) side-channel analysis of the Python implementation, (3) exploration of hardware entropy sources for the OTP pad to achieve true (not pseudo) randomness, and (4) investigation of whether the Lorenz extraction function can be replaced by a standard stream cipher (e.g., ChaCha20) without losing the physically-motivated design intent.

7. Conclusion

We have presented FSC, a seven-layer cryptographic construction that integrates physical simulation, chaotic dynamics, and the One-Time Pad into a single encryption pipeline. The construction achieves empirically near-ideal ciphertext entropy (7.997 bits), strong avalanche properties via the Lorenz butterfly effect (99.6% keystream divergence from a 10^{-10} perturbation), and Shannon perfect secrecy at the outermost layer.

FSC is a research vehicle rather than a production cipher. Its value lies not in competing with AES or ChaCha20, but in demonstrating that physically-motivated mathematical structures — differential equations, nuclear decay kinetics, fractal geometry — can be formally instantiated as cipher components and combined with provably secure primitives. The construction raises an open question: can a multi-domain cipher be designed such that each domain layer independently satisfies a cryptographic hardness assumption, yielding a construction whose total security is the product of independent hardness assumptions rather than the minimum?
